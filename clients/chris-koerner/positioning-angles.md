# Chris Koerner -- Positioning Angles

**Source:** Brief extracted from "How to Buy Big Things with Little Money" (The Koerner Office)
**Purpose:** 5 positioning angles for Starter tier deliverables (lead magnet, blog post, newsletter, landing page, 10 LinkedIn posts)
**Primary CTA:** Newsletter signup (tkopod.com)
**Secondary CTA:** TKOwners community ($99/month)

---

## Market Context

The "buy businesses/real estate with no money" space is Stage 3-4 on Schwartz's sophistication scale. The audience has heard "no money down" since the 1980s. They've seen the YouTube gurus. They've read Rich Dad Poor Dad. They're skeptical of promises and tired of theory.

What breaks through at this stage: **mechanism** (show me HOW, specifically), **identity** (speak to who I am, not just what I want), and **proof** (show me you've actually done this, with real numbers from real deals).

Chris Koerner has all three. The positioning challenge isn't inventing credibility -- it's choosing which slice of his deep experience to lead with for each deliverable.

---

## Angle 1: The Strongest Hook

**"He bought his first house making $2.13 an hour."**

- **The angle:** The gap between $2.13/hour waiter wages and homeownership is so absurd it stops the scroll and forces a second look -- and the fact that he did it during the 2008 financial crisis makes it undeniable.
- **Why it works:** Specificity is the enemy of skepticism. "$2.13/hour" is not a round number. It's not motivational fluff. It's the federal tipped minimum wage, and anyone who's waited tables knows exactly what that means. The brain can't dismiss a claim this specific -- it has to find out how. This creates what copywriters call an "open loop" that demands resolution. The audience immediately self-sorts: "If he could do it on $2.13/hour, my situation isn't as impossible as I thought."
- **Headline direction:**
  - "I Bought My First House Making $2.13 an Hour. Here's the Playbook I Used."
  - "$2.13/Hour. Newlywed. 2008 Financial Crisis. How I Bought a House Anyway."
  - "The $2.13/Hour Deal Structure That Changed Everything"
- **When to use it:** Lead with this on **LinkedIn posts** (hooks that stop the scroll in a feed full of humble-brags), **blog post headline/intro** (search-driven content needs a compelling open), and any **paid social ads** if Chris ever runs them. This is the "get attention" angle -- it earns the right to teach.

## Angle 2: The Contrarian Take

**"Having no money isn't your problem -- it's your biggest advantage."**

- **The angle:** The conventional wisdom says you need capital to acquire assets. Chris's central thesis flips this: constraints are a forcing function for creativity, and bootstrapped startups have statistically the same success rate as venture-funded ones. Money makes you lazy. Broke makes you resourceful.
- **Why it works:** This directly attacks the audience's core limiting belief -- "I can't start because I don't have money." Instead of validating the excuse (which every guru does before selling their course), Chris reframes the constraint as the advantage itself. This is a pattern interrupt. The audience expects sympathy; they get a challenge. It also triggers what psychologists call "cognitive reappraisal" -- the moment someone recategorizes a threat as an opportunity, their entire behavioral response shifts. They move from paralysis to action. The contrarian frame also positions Chris against the VC/capital-first crowd, which resonates with the bootstrap-minded audience he's building for.
- **Headline direction:**
  - "Stop Waiting for Capital. It's Making You Worse at Deal-Making."
  - "Why I'm Against Raising Money (And What to Do Instead)"
  - "Constraints Equal Creativity: The Case Against Waiting Until You Can Afford It"
  - "The Broke Entrepreneur's Unfair Advantage"
- **When to use it:** This is the **newsletter** angle. A newsletter edition built around this contrarian reframe gives readers an "aha" moment and a reason to share. Also strong for **LinkedIn thought leadership posts** (the 3-4 posts in the mix that are meant to provoke and position). This angle is too confrontational for a landing page (where you need to build trust, not challenge), but it's perfect for content that earns attention and sparks conversation.

## Angle 3: The Data-Backed Claim

**"10 deal structures. Real numbers. From someone who's bought and sold five 7-8 figure companies."**

- **The angle:** This isn't theory from a textbook or a coach who's "studied" deal-making. Chris provides 10 specific seller financing structures -- balloon payments, graduated payments, master leases, sub-2, partial carrybacks, shared appreciation mortgages, asset swaps, delayed down payments, zero-down structures, and interest-only arrangements -- each with actual dollar amounts from deals he's personally done or directly participated in. $800K business for $50K down. $2M RV park for $7K/month lease. $200K landscaping business flipped to $600K. $63K scaled to $4.3M monthly revenue.
- **Why it works:** At Stage 3-4 market sophistication, claims without proof get ignored. The audience has been burned by people selling "secrets" that turn out to be surface-level advice. Specificity is proof. When someone says "10 structures" instead of "multiple strategies," and backs each one with a dollar amount from a real deal, the credibility gap closes. The sheer volume of structures also creates a completeness signal -- "this person has seen enough deals to have 10 different tools, not just one trick." The real numbers function as social proof without needing testimonials. The deals themselves are the testimony.
- **Headline direction:**
  - "10 Ways to Buy a Business With Little or No Money Down (With Real Numbers)"
  - "The Deal Structure Playbook: 10 Seller Financing Methods I've Used Across 5 Companies"
  - "$50K Down on an $800K Business. $7K/Month Lease on a $2M Property. Here Are the 10 Structures That Make It Possible."
- **When to use it:** This is the **blog post** angle and the **lead magnet** angle. For the blog post: structure it as a comprehensive guide targeting search terms like "how to buy a business with no money" or "seller financing structures." Each structure gets its own section with real numbers -- this is the kind of content Google rewards and AI search cites. For the lead magnet: a downloadable checklist or cheat sheet of all 10 structures with the key negotiation phrases for each. The data-backed credibility also belongs in the **landing page** proof section (not the headline, but the "why trust this guy" section).

## Angle 4: The Story Angle

**"Two broke college kids, $2.13/hour, 2008 financial crisis -- and they bought two houses in 9 months."**

- **The angle:** Chris and his wife's origin story -- newlyweds in Alabama, waiting tables at a Thai restaurant, buying their first house with government grants during the worst financial crisis in a generation, then using the $8,000 Obama tax credit as a down payment on a rental property 9 months later -- is the emotional engine for everything else. One resourceful move cascading into the next. The refusing-to-pay-PMI story adds the character dimension: this is someone who reads the fine print, pushes back on authority, and gets what he asks for because he's willing to hear "no" and keep going.
- **Why it works:** Stories bypass the skepticism filter that data triggers. The audience doesn't evaluate a story for accuracy the way they evaluate a claim -- they experience it. Chris's origin story works on three emotional levels simultaneously. First, relatability: most of his audience has been broke, and the $2.13/hour detail makes it visceral. Second, agency: Chris didn't get lucky or inherit money -- he read about government programs, negotiated with the bank, and turned one asset into two. The audience sees a playbook, not a miracle. Third, escalation: the progression from $90K house to rental property to five 7-8 figure companies creates a "if he started there and got here" narrative arc that makes the audience's own starting point feel less like a disqualifier. The PMI negotiation story specifically models the behavior Chris wants his audience to adopt: ask for what you want, know what you're entitled to, and don't accept the first "no."
- **Headline direction:**
  - "How Two Broke College Students Bought Two Houses in 9 Months on $2.13/Hour"
  - "The $8,000 Check That Changed Our Lives (And How We Almost Missed It)"
  - "The Bank Said I Had to Pay PMI. I Said No. Here's What Happened."
  - "From $2.13/Hour to Five 7-Figure Companies: The First Deal That Started It All"
- **When to use it:** This is the **newsletter** opening and the **landing page** hero/origin section. Lead with the story, then transition to the frameworks and structures. On LinkedIn, use the PMI negotiation as a standalone **story-based post** -- it's self-contained, has a clear conflict/resolution, and models behavior the audience can immediately apply. The full origin arc (first house through two properties in 9 months) is the emotional foundation for the **lead magnet intro** -- before you give them the 10 structures, show them what's possible when someone with nothing applies even one of them.

## Angle 5: The Urgency / Identity Angle

**"This is for people who are tired of waiting to 'have enough' to start."**

- **The angle:** The audience isn't beginners -- they're aspiring acquirers who already know they want to own assets but have been stuck in preparation mode, saving up, watching YouTube, reading books, telling themselves "once I have $X, I'll make my move." This angle speaks directly to that identity and the frustration of watching others move while they wait. The urgency isn't artificial (no countdown timer) -- it's existential: every month you wait is a month of deal flow you miss, a month of relationships you don't build, a month of compounding you don't capture.
- **Why it works:** Identity-based positioning is the most powerful angle at Stage 4 market sophistication because it doesn't ask the audience to believe a claim -- it asks them to recognize themselves. "Tired of waiting" isn't a feature or a benefit. It's a mirror. The audience who resonates with this language self-selects hard: they lean forward, they click, they subscribe. The ones who don't resonate simply scroll past, which is fine -- they weren't the right audience anyway. The urgency component works because it's true, not manufactured. The Mom vs. Stranger framework from Chris's content proves it: relationships take time to build, and the seller who's ready to do a creative deal today won't be on the market in 6 months. Every month of "preparing" is a month of missed relationship-building. Chris's own trajectory proves the cost of waiting -- his first house at 21 is now 17 years of real estate experience and five companies later. The compounding started because he moved before he was "ready."
- **Headline direction:**
  - "Stop Saving Up. Start Structuring Deals."
  - "You Don't Need More Money. You Need a Different Conversation With the Seller."
  - "For Aspiring Owners Who Are Done Watching From the Sidelines"
  - "The Waiting Is the Mistake: Why Your Next Deal Doesn't Require Your Next Dollar"
  - "You Already Know Enough to Make Your First Offer. Here's the Structure to Use."
- **When to use it:** This is the **landing page** headline angle and the **CTA language** across all deliverables. When someone finishes a blog post, the CTA isn't "subscribe for more tips" -- it's "Stop waiting. Get the playbook. Subscribe at tkopod.com." When someone finishes a LinkedIn post, the CTA is "If you're done preparing and ready to start structuring, the newsletter breaks down one deal like this every week." This angle also drives the **contrarian LinkedIn posts** -- the ones that challenge the "save up first" mindset and position Chris's community as the place where people who are ready to move actually move.

## Recommended Starting Point

**Lead with Angle 1 (the $2.13/hour hook) to get attention, then deliver on Angle 3 (the 10 structures with real numbers) to build credibility.**

The hook earns the click. The structures earn the trust. The story (Angle 4) provides the emotional through-line. The contrarian reframe (Angle 2) keeps people thinking about it after they close the tab. And the identity angle (Angle 5) drives every CTA: "Stop waiting. Start structuring."

This sequencing mirrors Chris's own content style -- he leads with a specific, surprising detail, backs it with frameworks and numbers, and closes with a challenge to act.
